ABSTRACT

Melt differential centrifugal electrospinning (MDCE) method is proposed by integrating the advantages of centrifugal spinning and melt differential electrospinning, including high efficiency, solvent-free, multiple jets formation from nozzle-less spinning system, and small diameter. A mathematical model of jet diameter in MDCE is established. An orthogonal experiment is carried out to explore the effects of main processing parameters on the average diameter and the diameter standard deviation of the prepared fibers. Ultimately, polypropylene (PP) nanofibers with an average diameter of 790 nm are successfully prepared in a higher flow rate of 124.26 g h−1 than that of other methods. The X-ray diffraction and differential scanning calorimeter indicate that the introduction of high-voltage electrostatic field in centrifugal spinning contribute to the crystal orientation of the PP molecular chain. Therefore, tensile mechanical strength is enhanced as the increase of the loading voltage. MDCE may provide an efficient and eco-friendly method for nanofiber manufacturing in the future. © 2019 Wiley Periodicals, Inc. J. Appl. Polym. Sci. 2020, 137, 48299.

INTRODUCTION

At present, the main methods for nanofiber preparation are self-assembly, template, phase separation, electrospinning, and so forth.1-4 Electrospinning has gained much attention due to its facile equipment and process. However, it is difficult to achieve high efficiency preparation of nanofibers because of the limit flow rate in the process. In order to improve the nanofiber production efficiency, some researchers turned to the centrifugal spinning.5-7 Up to now, centrifugal spinning mainly uses polymer solutions as raw material, in which the spinning solutions have large content of toxic solvents. The use of toxic solvents may cause potential environmental pollution, which limits its development. Using polymer melt as the spinning material could avoid the problem effectively, so some researchers attempted to melt centrifugal spinning.8-12

Fang et al.11 prepared tin fluorophosphate glass fibers with average diameters ranging from 2 to 4 μm via centrifugal forcespinning. In the melt centrifugal spinning process, the high-viscosity melt is only driven by the centrifugal force, thus the melt cannot be stretched sufficiently during the jet flow, which leads to insufficient fineness of fibers and inhomogeneous of diameter distribution. In order to increase the driving force in the spinning process, some researchers have tried combination of the melt electrospinning and the centrifugal spinning.13-15 Although the diameter of the fiber is refined, capillary structure has the disadvantages of low production efficiency. It still needs to decrease the diameter and increase the number of the capillary to increase production efficiency. Kancheva et al.16 used one to four nozzles for centrifugal electrospinning, and the efficiency increased to a certain extent. However, capillary with small diameter could be easily blocked and may cause difficulty in its cleaning. The certain distance between capillaries is necessarily kept to avoid the high voltage electric field interaction between capillaries that would also reduce the production efficiency of fibers.

To overcome the aforementioned challenges, we developed a novel melt differential centrifugal electrospinning (MDCE) method, in which the electrostatic field and self-designed centrifugal differential disk are introduced to generate multiple jets from the rim of the disk, and the purpose of reducing the fiber diameter and improving the uniformity and production efficiency are achieved. Therefore, MDCE has great potential in the field of high efficiency fine fibers fabrication. Moreover, through the mathematical model of jet path and orthogonal experiment and, we first determined the weight of each parameter on the average fiber diameter and its uniformity. It may provide a guidance for further research.

EXPERIMENTAL

Materials

The polypropylene (PP; Grade 6520; Mw 100,000, China) was purchased from Shanghai Expert in the Developing of New Material Co., Ltd, Shanghai, China. The melting temperature is 160 °C, and the melt flow index is 2000 g/10 min.

Equipment

A schematic of self-designed machine used for MDCE and its spinning process are shown in Figure 1. It is consisted of five main components: (1) high-speed motor, (2) microextruder, (3) electromagnetic heating device, (4) centrifugal differential disk, and (5) four electrode plates.

The power of high-speed motor is 12,000 W and the disk revolutions per minute (rpm) is up to 4000 rpm. The electromagnetic heating device is fixed under the centrifugal differential disk, and the temperature of the centrifugal differential disk is monitored by an infrared thermometer and could be set in the range of 150–300 °C. The diameter of the centrifugal differential disk is 200 mm, and the distance between the electrode plate and the edge of the centrifugal differential disk is 150 mm.

Orthogonal Experimental Design

According to the previous experiments, it was found that when the spinning temperature was below 200 °C, the melt viscosity was too high for the spinning condition, causing unstable flow and hence the large droplet When the spinning temperature was above 240 °C, oxidation and thermal degradation starts to occur as indicated by the yellowness of the fibers, and its strength was weak.17-19 So, we chose 200–240 °C as the spinning temperature. When the spinning disk rpm was less than 2000 rpm, the centrifugal force was too weak to consistently draw the melt into fiber, again causing unstable flow and the formation of large droplets. So, we chose the range of 2000–4000 rpm as the centrifugal differential disk speed. Moreover, when the spinning voltage was higher than 50 kV, the electrostatic breakdown would occur, and when the voltage was too low, the electrostatic field was too weak as the secondary fiber drawing force for continual and consistent fiber drawing. So, the spinning voltage was set around 25–45 kV. Finally, when the speed of the microextruder was above 30 rpm, there were a lot of droplets on the fibers, it would affect the application of fibers. Thus, in this study, the speed of the extruder was set at 10, 20, and 30 rpm, and the corresponding flow rate was set at 124.26, 152.28, and 192.96 g h−1.

Four factors in MDCE process, centrifugal differential disk speed, extruder speed, spinning voltage and spinning temperature, were investigated follow the Taguchi orthogonal experimental method. The effects of these factors on the fiber morphology were investigated. Three levels parameters of orthogonal experimental in MDCE are shown in Table 1.

Preparation of Nanofibers

When the extruder and centrifugal differential disk were heated to the set temperature and the temperature was stable, the PP was added to the hopper of the extruder. The screw extruded the melt into the centrifugal differential disk, when the PP was fully melted. The melt flow rate could be controlled by adjusting the speed of the extruder. After that, the high-speed motor was turned on to drive the centrifugal differential disk. When the speed of the centrifugal differential disk reached to the set value, the high voltage electrostatic generator was turned on and increased to the set value. The spinning process is illustrated in Figure 1. As it was shown, the melt entered to the centrifugal differential disk through the extruder, and self-differentiated by the electric field force. With the traction of centrifugal force and electric field force, multiple jets formed along the zigzag slot on the edge of the centrifugal differential disk. The jets were further stretched by the electric field force during the flight and solidified into fibers.

Characterization

The morphology of fibers was observed by a scanning electron microscopy (SEM; HITACHI S4700; Hitachi, Tokyo, Japan) with an accelerated voltage of 10 kV.

The fiber diameters were measured from SEM images using the ImageJ software (National Institutes of Health, Bethesda, MD). Fifty random fibers were selected from 10 images for measurement of average diameter and standard deviation.

The crystallinity of fibers was characterized by X-ray diffraction (XRD; D-max2500; PIGAKV, Rigaku, Tokyo, Japan). The fibers were made into samples, in which surface area was 1 × 2 cm2 and thickness was 2 mm. The samples were measured in wide angle, and the angle was set for 5–90°.

The crystallinity of fibers was assessed by differential scanning calorimeter (DSC; DSC-204F, NETZSCH, Selb, Germany). About 5 mg of the samples were sealed in aluminum pan and were heated from 25 to 200 °C at a rate of 10 °C min−1.

For investigation of tensile strength of fibers, about 10 mg of the fibers were twisted into a cylinder about 3 mm in diameter and 20 mm in length, clamped on the nanotensile testing system (Aglient T150 UTM), the load value at start was 750 μN and the extension speed was 2E-4 mm s−1.

The fineness of fiber can direct the application of fiber to a large extent. As shown in Table 2, the minimum average diameter of fibers in the seventh group is 790 nm, and the experimental condition was A3B1C3D2, in which the speed of the centrifugal differential disk was 4000 rpm, the speed of the extruder was 10 rpm, the spinning voltage was 45 kV, and the spinning temperature was 220 °C. The maximum centrifugal and electric forces, indeed of the minimum of feeding rate could stretch the jet more and forming the fineness fibers.

The maximum average diameter of fibers was 8.09 μm, the experimental condition was A1B3C3D3, in which the speed of the centrifugal differential disk was 2000 rpm, the speed of the extruder was 30 rpm, the spinning voltage was 45 kV, and the spinning temperature was 240 °C. In this group, although the voltage value was set to maximum value, due to the minimum value of centrifugal force and the maximum feed rate, the jet is less stretched and resulted in thicker fibers.

It could be seen from Figure 6 that fibers prepared by MCDE had smooth surface with no beads and pores. In addition, there were many nanofibers near the micrometer size, in which the smallest diameter of nanofibers was 160 nm for Group 7, as it is shown in Figure 7. It could indicate that the MDCE could fabricate the nanosized ultrafine fibers with a diameter of ~100 nm. Comparing the range R of the factors in Table 2, the factors affecting the average diameter of fibers were ranked as follows: centrifugal differential disk speed (4.86) > extruder speed (2.63) > spinning voltage (0.90) > spinning temperature (0.55). The influence of centrifugal differential disk speed and extruder speed was much higher than the other two items. The speed of centrifugal differential disk determined the centrifugal force of the melt and the centrifugal force directly affected the stretching of the fiber. So, the speed of centrifugal differential disk had a significant effect on fiber's diameter. At the same time, the speed of the extruder affected the flow rate of the melt. When the extruder speed was lower, the less melt flowed into the centrifugal differential disk. That caused the resultant of the force of polymer inter molecules became smaller, so the fiber was stretched more in the same external force and the average diameter of fibers became smaller. It can be declared that the extruder speed had a significant influence on the fiber diameter after centrifugal force. The effect of increasing the spinning temperature and voltage was similar. When the spinning in the higher voltage, the electric field force became larger and could stretch the jet more. It promoted fibers stretching and made the diameter of the fibers smaller. The melt viscosity was low, when the spinning temperature was high. It reduced the intermolecular force and increased the melt fluidity. So, these two factors indirectly promoted the refinement of fibers, too. The experimental results were in agreement with the previous theoretical results. And the experimental results verified the correctness of the mathematical model of jet path.

Standard Deviation Analysis of Diameter

The distribution of fiber diameter was an important index to evaluate the fiber uniformity. Table 3 indicates the influence of four factors on the standard deviation of fibers prepared by MDCE. The standard deviation could reflect the distribution of fibers diameter. The minimum standard deviation of the diameter was 0.38 μm in which fiber distribution was the most uniform. Moreover, the spinning conditions were the same as those with the finest diameter. Thus, the fibers were the most excellent in this condition. Comparing the R values of various factors, the factors affecting the standard deviation of fibers were ranked as follows: centrifugal differential disk speed (0.79) > spinning voltage (0.50) > extruder speed (0.34) > spinning temperature (0.28). The speed of the centrifugal differential disk had the greatest influence on the fiber diameter distribution. Because when the speed of the centrifugal differential disk was larger, the fibers were generally stretched enough, and the diameter of the fibers was evenly distributed. The influence of spinning voltage on the distribution of fiber diameter took the second place because the external electric field could promote the self-formation of multiple jets and made the fibers self-differentiation before leaving centrifugal differential disk. Due to the existence of the uniform electric field, the homogenization effect of each part of the melt was the same. Therefore, the electric field had a significant effect on the homogenization effect of the fibers. At the same time, the purpose of improving product efficiency was achieved. The comparison of production efficiency and diameter was shown in Table 4. The spinning efficiency of MDCE was obviously higher than that of other methods.

The Effect of Spinning Voltage on the Crystalline Properties of Fibers

The mechanical properties of fibers could be evaluated according to the crystallinity. The higher crystallinity of fibers corresponding to the greater fiber elastic modulus. Therefore, increasing the crystallinity of the fibers could improve its mechanical properties.

Figure 8 is the SEM images at lower magnification of the fibers prepared at different spinning voltages at the MDCE condition; A:3, B:1, and D:2. It could be observed that when the spinning voltage was 0 kV, due to the absence of electric field force, the uniformity of the fibers was poor and there were droplets. When there was an external electric field, the uniformity of the fibers gradually improved and the droplets gradually decreased. When spinning voltage was 45 kV, due to the sufficiently stretching of electric field, the uniformity of the fibers is the best, and the fibers have a certain degree of orientation. The high orientation refers to the high crystallinity and the high mechanical property.

Figure 9 is the XRD result of fibers prepared at different spinning voltages at the MDCE condition: A:3, B:1, and D:2. From the diagram, it could be observed that when the spinning voltage was 0 kV, which was just having the centrifugal spinning, the crystallization peak of the fibers was in the weakest point. At this time, the mechanical properties of the fibers were weak. When there was an external electric field, the fibers crystallization peak increased and with the increased of spinning voltage, the crystallization peak of fibers was more acute. When the applied voltage reached the maximum value of 45 kV, the crystallization peak intensity was the largest, and compared with the spinning voltage of 35 kV, the crystallization peak increased significantly, and the mechanical properties of the fibers were enhanced. This is the reason of the external electric field which causes the fibers more stretching and better intermolecular orientation and crystallinity could be obtained in fiber axis. The electric field force had the effect of stretching and drawing on the melt jet, which made the fibers molecule arranged in the direction of drawing more orderly and improved the crystallinity of the fibers. Furthermore, Figure 10 is the DSC result of fibers prepared at different spinning voltages. From the diagram, it could be found that when the spinning voltage was 0 kV, the area of crystallization peak was the smallest. With the increase of spinning voltage, the area of crystallization peak increases gradually. When the spinning voltage was 45 kV, the area of crystallization peak was the largest. We calculated the values of crystallinity from the ratio of the area under peak to melting enthalpy. The melting enthalpy of PP at 100% crystallization is 209 J g−1. The crystallinity of fibers prepared at 0–45 kV was 18.1, 19.6, 24.9, and 28.7%, respectively. The increase of crystallinity of fibers enhanced the tensile strength of the fibers, as the results of the tensile mechanical properties of fibers are shown in Table 5. The tensile stress and modulus of the fiber were the largest at 45 kV, which were 702 MPa and 3.760 GPa.

CONCLUSIONS

A mathematical model of jet path in centrifugal electrospinning was established. The fine PP fibers with an average diameter of 790 nm were successfully prepared by the self-designed MDCE machine, which was nearly twice finer than the fibers prepared by centrifugal spinning, and the diameter distribution uniformity increased by 22.45%.8 The finest fiber diameter was 160 nm. During the spinning process, the extruder speed, the differential centrifugal disk speed, and spinning voltage were the main key factors affecting the average fibers diameter. Therefore, the diameter of fibers can be refined by reducing the speed of extruder and increasing the speed of centrifugal differential disk in practical production, the fiber can be more uniform by increasing the speed of centrifugal differential disk and spinning voltage. Moreover, external electric field improved the crystallinity of the fibers. Apart from this, within a certain range, the mechanical properties of fibers could be improved by increasing the spinning voltage. In production, ultrafine fibers with different characteristics could be easily prepared by changing the corresponding parameters. This experiment provided an experimental reference for the industrialization of centrifugal electrospinning.
